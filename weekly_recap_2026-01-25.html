CORPORATE FINANCE

CORPORATE FINANCE - Session 5
January 19 | Estimating Cost of Capital (Walmart Case)

KEY CONCEPTS

  • WACC: Weighted Average Cost of Capital - the hurdle rate for capital investments
  • Cost of Debt (Kd): Based on yield to maturity, NOT coupon rate
  • Cost of Equity (Ke): Required return by shareholders using CAPM
  • Beta: Measure of stock volatility relative to market (systematic risk)

KEY FORMULAS

CAPM: Ke = Rf + β × (Market Return - Rf)

WACC: WACC = wd × Kd × (1-t) + we × Ke

Bloomberg Adjusted Beta: 0.67 × Raw Beta + 0.33
WALMART CASE DATA

KEY DISTINCTIONS

KEY INSIGHT

Cost of capital determines which projects create shareholder value. Use YTM because it reflects current market rates, not historical coupon rates.

CORPORATE FINANCE - Session 6
January 20 | Capital Structure (American Home Products Case)

THE BIG PICTURE

Trade-off Theory: Value = Unlevered Firm + PV(Tax Benefits) - PV(Bankruptcy Costs)

Optimal Capital Structure: The debt/equity mix that minimizes WACC → maximizes firm value

KEY FORMULAS

Kd = Rf + Corporate Spread (spread depends on rating)

Ke = Rf + βL × ERP (levered beta increases with debt)

Interest Coverage Ratio = EBIT / Interest Expense → determines rating
AMERICAN HOME PRODUCTS CASE
Situation: AHP had NO debt despite being highly profitable
Why? Conservative culture, risk avoidance, no need for external financing
Analysis:
  • At 30% debt: Very low risk (interest coverage 2x Warner-Lambert even at 50%)
  • Stock price gain from leverage minimal when PE already 30x
  • Implementation: Special dividend preferred (institutions are tax-exempt)

Key Insight: AHP illustrates the limits of corporate finance theory - culture and management preferences matter

TARGET DEBT RATIO ASSESSMENT

  1. RISKS: Internal constraints, leverage risks, interest coverage
  2. SIGNALS: Bond ratings, interest rates, PE ratios, market access
  3. COMPARISONS: Industry benchmarks

WACC AND OPTIMAL DEBT

As leverage increases → WACC decreases (tax shield + lower Kd) → At optimal point, WACC minimized → Beyond optimal: bankruptcy costs increase, WACC rises again.

January 19 | Ferric Processing Case - Regression Analysis

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Linear Regression: Y = a + bX + error
  • Non-Linear Regression: When linear models show patterns in residuals
  • Residual Analysis: Check for random distribution to verify assumptions
  • Economies of Scale: Inverse relationship between capacity and average cost
FERRIC PROCESSING CASE
Question: What's the relationship between plant capacity and average cost?

MODEL COMPARISON

CriterionLinear ModelNon-Linear Model
EquationCost = 25.19 - 0.008×CapacityCost = 11.74 + 3962/Capacity
Adjusted R²0.660.94
Standard Error2.330.98
Residual Pattern?Yes (problem!)No (random)
2000-ton Prediction$9.19 (underestimates)$13.72 (accurate)

WHY NON-LINEAR MAKES SENSE

Total Cost = Fixed Cost + Variable Cost
Average Cost = Total Cost / Capacity = Fixed Cost/Capacity + Unit Cost
Therefore: Cost = a + b/Capacity (hyperbolic relationship)
Where: a = unit cost, b = fixed cost

REGRESSION ANALYSIS STEPS

  1. Visually inspect data with scatter plots
  2. Check correlations (CORREL function)
  3. Develop model and interpret equation
  4. Examine significance (p-value < 0.05)
  5. Check residual plots for patterns
  6. Add/remove variables based on significance
  7. Use model for forecasting

MULTICOLLINEARITY

  • Definition: When independent variables are highly correlated (r > 0.9)
  • Problem: Makes coefficients unreliable
  • Solution: Drop one of the correlated variables

HERTZ RISK ANALYSIS MODEL

  • Most likely return

KEY CONCEPTS

LEAN vs AGILE vs LEAGILE

Lean
  • High volume, low variety
  • Predictable environments
  • Focus: minimize cost
Agile
  • High variety/variability
  • Unpredictable environments
  • Focus: speed, flexibility

Leagile: Combined strategy using decoupling point

SPORT OBERMEYER CASE
Problem: Must commit 50% of production before demand is known
Solution: Use forecast variance to sequence production
Operational improvements:
  • Reduce lead times (materials & production)
  • Increase reactive capacity
  • Reduce minimum order quantities
  • Obtain market information earlier
Safety Factor: If Expected Demand > 2x MOQ → produce at least 1 MOQ first
SEVEN-ELEVEN JAPAN CASE
Strategy: Micro-match supply and demand via rapid replenishment
Store location: Dense clusters of 50-60 stores with corresponding DCs
Distribution: All products through DCs (no direct store delivery)
Information: Real-time sales analysis for short-term demand prediction
Result: Reduced truck visits from 70/day (1974) to 9/day (2006)

KEY INSIGHT

Competitive advantage comes from aligning product strategy with supply chain design, not from applying one "best practice" to all products.

MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS

MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS - Session 4
January 20 | Price Controls

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Consumer Surplus: Value consumers derive minus what they pay
  • Producer Surplus: Revenue minus minimum acceptable price
  • Deadweight Loss: Economic inefficiency from price controls
  • Price Floor: Minimum price above equilibrium (e.g., minimum wage)

PRICE FLOOR ANALYSIS

A = Gain to sellers (higher price)
B = Loss to sellers (reduced quantity sold)
Net effect = A - B (depends on elasticity)
Creates excess supply (unemployment in labor markets)

ELASTICITY IMPACT ON MINIMUM WAGE

Labor DemandEffect on WorkersWhy?
InelasticWorkers gain on net (A > B)Few job losses, higher wages
ElasticWorkers lose on net (B > A)Many job losses outweigh wage gains
UBER CASE
Consumer Surplus: Estimated at $7 billion
Price Elasticity: -0.6 (inelastic demand)
Surge Pricing: Reveals true demand curve through behavior
Pareto Solution: Compensate medallion owners (~$1B loss) from surplus generated

MINIMUM WAGE DATA

JurisdictionRateNotes
Federal (US)$7.25/hourUnchanged since 2009
California Fast-Food$20/hourApril 2024

Empirical Evidence: Short-run elasticity = -0.28 (inelastic) | Long-run = -1.5 (elastic)

ALTERNATIVE: UNIVERSAL BASIC INCOME (UBI)

  • Advantage: Doesn't interfere with wage-setting, avoids deadweight loss
  • Caveat: May reduce work incentives
  • Pareto Superior: No job losses, direct support for poor

KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • Government policies have redistributive effects AND efficiency costs
  • Pareto-efficient alternatives often exist
  • Whether policies benefit intended beneficiaries is an empirical question

MANAGERIAL ACCOUNTING

MANAGERIAL ACCOUNTING - Session 3
January 20 | Building a Costing System

5 BUILDING BLOCKS OF COSTING

  1. Cost Object: Anything for which we desire a cost
  2. Direct Costs: Can be traced economically to cost object
  3. Indirect Costs: Cannot be traced, must be allocated
  4. Cost Pool: Grouping of indirect cost items
  5. Cost Allocation Base: Systematic link between indirect costs and cost objects

JOB COSTING vs PROCESS COSTING

Job CostingProcess Costing
Distinct products/servicesMass-produced, indistinct items
Construction, advertising, vaccinesOil refining, juices, mining

JOB COSTING STEPS

  1. Identify cost object (job)
  2. Identify direct costs (materials, labor)
  3. Determine cost pools
  4. Select allocation base
  5. Compute rate: Budgeted OH / Budgeted Activity
  6. Allocate: Rate × Actual Activity
  7. Total Cost = Direct + Indirect

KEY FORMULAS

Budgeted MOH Rate = Budgeted Manufacturing Overhead / Budgeted Allocation Base

Allocated OH = Budgeted Rate × Actual Activity
CALDWELL TOYS CASE
Budgeted indirect rate: $5,100,000 / 150,000 DLH = $34/DLH
Actual indirect rate: $5,355,000 / 153,000 DLH = $35/DLH
Steel Wheels job cost (normal): $78,290 + $25,445 + (840 × $34) = $132,295

NORMAL vs ACTUAL COSTING

MethodUsesPreferred?
NormalBudgeted rate × actual activityYes
ActualActual rate × actual activityNo

Why normal preferred? Avoids fluctuation, enables timely costing

BGS - Session 4
January 21 | Xstrata Mining Tax Reform

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Sovereign Risk: Risk that government policy changes harm investment returns
  • Non-Market Strategy: Actions to influence government policy
  • Political Environment Analysis: Understanding political conditions affecting business
XSTRATA CASE
Company: Swiss-headquartered mining company, 33% of assets in Australia
Issue: Australian government announced mining tax reform (RSPT)
Impact: Current effective tax rate: 40% → Proposed: >55%
Investment at risk: A$45 billion invested 2002-2010

STRATEGIC OPTIONS

  1. Lobbying: Persuade government to modify tax proposal
  2. Coalition Building: Ally with other mining companies (BHP, Rio Tinto)
  3. Geographic Diversification: Shift investments elsewhere
  4. Operational Adjustment: Suspend Australian operations

KEY CONSIDERATIONS FOR FOREIGN MULTINATIONALS

  • Legitimacy concerns when intervening in domestic policy
  • Coalition with domestic companies increases influence
  • Need careful stakeholder management

KEY INSIGHT

Australia was considered low sovereign risk - but elected governments can still change policies significantly.

BGS - Session 5
January 22 | International Trade Policy (Chiquita Case)

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Trade Policy: Government regulations on imports/exports
  • Protectionism: Policies that restrict foreign competition
  • Multilateral vs. Bilateral Trade: WTO rules vs. regional agreements
CHIQUITA BRANDS CASE
Company: World's largest banana distributor
Issue: EU banana regime (1993) caused significant market share loss
Impact: $128M profit (1991) → consecutive losses by 1994
Stock: Dropped from $40 to $13.63

HISTORICAL CONTEXT: UNITED FRUIT COMPANY

  • "El Pulpo" (The Octopus) - dominant force in Latin America
  • Controlled land, transportation, political connections
  • "Banana republics" - disproportionate corporate power

EU BANANA REGIME ISSUES

  • Discriminatory against Latin American producers
  • Favored former European colonies (ACP countries)
  • Quota system limited Chiquita's market access

STRATEGIC RESPONSES

  1. WTO dispute resolution
  2. Political lobbying in US and EU
  3. Operational restructuring
  4. Market diversification

KEY INSIGHT

Even free-market democracies can impose protectionist policies that harm multinationals.
