CORPORATE FINANCE - Session 3

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Asset Liquidation: Consider tax impact (gain/loss × tax rate)
  • Sunk Costs: Past costs irrelevant for decisions - only consider differential cash flows
  • Project Comparability: Must compare risk, size, AND time horizon

FORMULAS

FCF = EBIT × (1-tax) + Depreciation - Capex - ΔWorking Capital

Tax Impact on Liquidation = (Liquidation Value - Book Value) × Tax Rate

Risk-Adjusted Discount Rate = Base Rate × (1 + Risk Premium)

CORPORATE FINANCE - Session 4
January 13 | Cost of Capital Determination (WACC)

THE BIG PICTURE

Goal: Find the debt/equity mix that minimizes WACC → maximizes firm value

Modigliani-Miller: In perfect markets, capital structure doesn't matter. But real-world imperfections (taxes, bankruptcy costs) create an optimal capital structure.

KEY FORMULAS

WACC = wd × Kd × (1-t) + we × Ke

CAPM: Ke = Rf + β × ERP

Unlevered Beta: βasset = βlevered / [1 + (1-t)(D/E)]

Levered Beta: βlevered = βasset × [1 + (1-t)(D/E)]

COMPONENTS BREAKDOWN

Equity Cost (Ke)
  • Rf: Risk-free rate (gov't bond matching project horizon)
  • Beta: Systematic risk measure (cov/var)
  • ERP: Equity Risk Premium (5.5-7% for US)
Debt Cost (Kd)
  • Government interest rates
  • Credit rating (S&P, Moody's, Fitch)
  • Credit spreads by rating

KEY INSIGHT

As leverage increases → both debt and equity become riskier → investors demand higher returns → eventually WACC rises again. The optimal point is where WACC is minimized.

DATA ANALYTICS - Session 2
January 12 | Regression Analysis Fundamentals

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Causal Methods: Using related variables to forecast (advertising → sales)
  • Correlation: Measures strength of linear relationship (-1 to +1)
  • Regression: Models relationship between dependent (Y) and independent (X) variables

FORMULAS & TOOLS

Correlation: =CORREL(Y, X) in Excel
Linear Regression: Y = Intercept + Coefficient × X + error
95% Confidence Interval: Mean ± 2 × Standard Error
Significance threshold: p-value < 0.05

5 STEPS TO INTERPRET REGRESSION OUTPUT

  1. F-significance < 0.05? → Model is significant
  2. Coefficient p-value < 0.05? → Variable is significant
  3. Write the equation (Y = a + bX)
  4. Check Adjusted R² → % variance explained
  5. Examine residual plot → Should be random (no patterns)

KEY INSIGHT

Correlation ≠ Causation. Always verify regression assumptions before making predictions.

January 13 | Non-Linear Regression (Ferric Processing Case)
FERRIC PROCESSING CASE
Question: What's the relationship between plant capacity and average cost?

MODEL COMPARISON

CriterionLinear ModelNon-Linear Model
EquationCost = 25.19 - 0.008×CapacityCost = 11.74 + 3962/Capacity
Adjusted R²0.660.94
Standard Error2.330.98
Residual Pattern?Yes (problem!)No (random)

WHY NON-LINEAR MAKES SENSE

Total Cost = Fixed Cost + Variable Cost
Average Cost = Total Cost / Capacity = Fixed Cost/Capacity + Unit Cost
Therefore: Cost = a + b/Capacity (hyperbolic relationship)

MULTIPLE REGRESSION & MULTICOLLINEARITY

  • Adding "Age" variable: p-value > 0.05 when capacity included (not significant)
  • Multicollinearity: When independent variables are highly correlated (r > 0.9)
  • Solution: Drop one of the correlated variables

STEPWISE REGRESSION

  • Forward Selection: Start with one predictor, add significant ones
  • Backward Elimination: Start with all, remove insignificant ones

COST CLASSIFICATION FRAMEWORK

ClassificationCategoriesKey Question
BehaviorVariable vs FixedHow does cost change with output?
Cost ObjectDirect vs IndirectCan it be traced to a cost object?

COST BEHAVIOR

Variable Costs
  • Total: Changes with output
  • Per unit: Unchanged
  • Ex: Direct materials, direct labor
Fixed Costs
  • Total: Unchanged
  • Per unit: Decreases with volume
  • Ex: Rent, depreciation

DIRECT vs INDIRECT

  • Direct: Can be traced conveniently & economically to cost object
  • Indirect: Must be allocated using a cost driver
  • Cost Driver: Whatever generates a cost (cause & effect)

COST OF GOODS SOLD SCHEDULE

Beginning DM Inventory
+ Purchases of DM
- Ending DM Inventory
= Direct Materials Used
+ Direct Labor
+ Manufacturing Overhead
= Cost of Goods Manufactured
+ Beginning Finished Goods
- Ending Finished Goods
= Cost of Goods Sold
THE BIG EVENT (TBE) CASE
Fixed costs per event: Music £10,000 + Catering fee £4,000 = £14,000
Variable costs per guest: Food £65 + Setup £15 - Discount £5 = £75/guest
Total Cost = £14,000 + (£75 × guests)
With 150 guests: £25,250 total (£168.33/guest)
With 200 guests: £29,000 total (£145.00/guest)
Key insight: Unit cost decreases with volume (fixed cost spread)

KEY CAVEAT

Use unit costs cautiously! Since unit costs change with output, base decisions on total dollars.

MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS

MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS - Sessions 2-3
January 14 | Supply, Demand & OLS Elasticity Estimation

PRICE ELASTICITY OF DEMAND

Definition: If price changes by 1%, how much does quantity demanded change (%)?

Formula: Elasticity = β₁ × (P/Q) at chosen point

WHY USE OLS REGRESSION?

Many factors affect demand: income, advertising, competitors, seasonality, weather...

OLS separates these influences so we can isolate the pure effect of price.

CONTROLLING FOR VARIABLES

Wrong model: Q = β₀ + β₁P (ignores other factors)
Correct model: Q = β₀ + β₁P + β₂Ad + β₃Income + ...

OMITTED VARIABLE BIAS - ICE CREAM EXAMPLE

Problem: Ice cream sells more in summer (high Q) AND you charge more (high P)
Without controlling for temperature: Regression thinks high P → high Q (wrong!)
Solution: Add temperature as a control variable to get true price elasticity

KEY INSIGHT

Without proper controls, elasticity estimates will be biased. Always include relevant demand shifters.

SUPPLY CHAIN - Session 2
January 12 | Bullwhip Effect, Collaboration & Integration

THE BULLWHIP EFFECT

Small demand fluctuations at consumer level become amplified as you move upstream in the supply chain.

  • Oscillation: Inventory/backlog cycles up and down
  • Amplification: Height of oscillations increases upstream
  • Lag: Swings happen later and increase upstream

4 ROOT CAUSES

CauseExplanationSolution
Demand Forecast UpdatingEach stage re-forecastsShare POS data (EDI)
Order BatchingBulk ordering increases variabilitySmaller, frequent orders
Price FluctuationsPromotions cause forward buyingEDLP (Everyday Low Pricing)
Rationing & Shortage GamingExaggerated orders during shortagesAllocate by past sales

FRAMEWORKS

  • VMI: Vendor-Managed Inventory
  • CPFR: Collaborative Planning, Forecasting & Replenishment
  • S&OP: Sales & Operations Planning
  • JITD: Just-In-Time Distribution
BARILLA CASE
Problem: Erratic distributor orders (SD = 227 quintals vs. mean of 300) despite stable consumer demand
Root causes: Volume discounts, promotions, no min/max order quantities, long lead times
JITD Proposal: Barilla manages distributor replenishment based on sell-through data
Barriers: Internal (sales incentives, loss of control) + External (distributor trust)
Outcome: Pilot programs succeeded → became classic VMI example

KEY INSIGHT

SCM is fundamentally about trust and collaboration, not just logistics optimization.
