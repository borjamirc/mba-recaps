CORPORATE FINANCE

CORPORATE FINANCE - Session 7
January 30 | The New Line Project (NPV & Incremental Cash Flows)

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Incremental Cash Flows: Only cash flows that change as a result of the project should be included in NPV analysis
  • Sunk Costs: Past expenditures irrelevant to future decisions (e.g., €135K research costs already spent)
  • Cannibalization: Sales erosion of existing products caused by the new product launch
  • Opportunity Cost: Value of the best alternative use of existing assets
THE NEW LINE PROJECT - Herramientas Industriales S.A.
Decision: Launch new DIY tool line extension to complement modern design line
Investment: €300,000 in molds and machinery
Hurdle Rate: 25% (owner's required return on equity)
Year1234
New Line Sales (€K)754812828828
Modern Line Uplift (€K)+19+124+253+310
Old Line Cannibalization (€K)-39-67-45-50
Margins: Old line 29% gross margin | Modern line 33% gross margin
Working Capital: 25% of sales (Yr 1) → 22% (Yr 2) → 20% (Yr 3+)
Tax Rate: 30%

CRITICAL ANALYSIS ISSUES

  1. Cannibalization MUST be included: Despite GM's objection, lost sales on existing products are real incremental cash flow impacts
  2. Research costs (€135K) are SUNK: Already spent regardless of decision — exclude from NPV
  3. Existing facilities: If capacity is truly idle with no alternative use, do NOT charge book/replacement value as investment
  4. General overheads (8-9% of sales): If they don't actually change with this project, do NOT allocate them
  5. Depreciation of existing assets: Not a relevant cash flow for this project decision
  6. Working capital: Include initial investment and annual changes; recover at project end
  7. Liquidation values: Include terminal values of new investment (€45K) at end of Year 4

KEY FORMULAS

Incremental Revenue = New Line Sales + Modern Line Uplift - Old Line Cannibalization

Incremental Margin = (New + Modern Uplift) × 33% - Old Cannibalization × 29%

Depreciation: €65K/yr (Yr 1-2) + €40K/yr (Yr 3-5)

Tax Shield = Depreciation × Tax Rate (30%)

NPV = Σ [Incremental After-Tax CF / (1.25)t] - Initial Investment

KEY INSIGHT

The case tests your ability to distinguish relevant from irrelevant costs. Each stakeholder (controller, operations manager, project managers, GM) pushes a different agenda. The correct NPV analysis includes only truly incremental cash flows.

DATA ANALYTICS FOR MANAGERS

DATA ANALYTICS - Session 6
January 26 | Risk Analysis in Capital Investment (Eagle Airlines - Continued)

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Risk Analysis Application: Building spreadsheet models to assess investment under uncertainty
  • Monte Carlo Simulation: Use probability distributions for each variable, run thousands of iterations
  • Sensitivity Analysis: Identify which variables have the greatest impact on profitability
  • Scenario Analysis: Test combinations of best/worst case assumptions
EAGLE AIRLINES - CONTINUED ANALYSIS
Decision: Purchase Piper Chieftain for $600,000 AUD
Key Parameters:
VariableLowExpectedHigh
Charter Mix60%-70%
Charter Price ($/hr)$1,900-$2,200
Ticket Price ($/person/hr)$240-$300
Utilization Rate50%60%70%
Flight Hours/Year7008001,000
Fixed: Operating costs $1,200/hr | Annual fixed costs $160,000 | Tax 33% | Discount rate 15%

SPREADSHEET MODELING APPROACH

  1. Revenue Model: Total Hours × [Charter% × Charter Price + (1-Charter%) × Seats × Utilization × Ticket Price]
  2. Cost Model: Total Hours × Operating Cost/hr + Annual Fixed Costs
  3. Profit Model: (Revenue - Costs - Depreciation) × (1 - Tax) + Depreciation
  4. NPV: Sum of discounted annual cash flows minus initial investment

HERTZ RISK ANALYSIS MODEL

Traditional approach: Single "expected" NPV
Problem: If each of 5 estimates has 60% chance of being correct:
Combined probability = 0.65 = 7.8%

Better approach: Generate probability distribution of returns
Shows: most likely return, range of outcomes, probability of loss

KEY DEPENDENCIES TO MODEL

  • Charter/ticket prices influenced by competitors (not fully controllable)
  • Utilization rate partly depends on ticket price (inverse relationship)
  • Flight hours depend on state of the economy
  • These correlations matter for simulation accuracy

KEY INSIGHT

A single-point NPV estimate can be dangerously misleading. Risk analysis reveals the full range of outcomes and helps identify which uncertainties matter most for the decision.

MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS

MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS - Session 5
January 26 | Externalities & Carbon Tax Policy

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Externalities: Costs or benefits imposed on third parties not involved in a transaction
  • Pigouvian Tax: Tax equal to the marginal external cost to internalize the externality
  • Carbon Pricing: Mechanism to make polluters pay for CO2 emissions (tax or cap-and-trade)
  • Cooperative Federalism: Federal government sets floor; provinces/states choose implementation
CANADA'S CARBON TAX
Context: Trudeau's Liberal Party implemented national carbon pricing in 2018
Design:
  • Two components: Fuel charge (consumers) + Output-Based Pricing System (industry)
  • Provincial flexibility: Each province designs own system OR takes federal backstop
  • Revenue stays local: All revenue returned to province of origin
  • Rising price: CA$10/ton (2018) → CA$50 (2022) → CA$170/ton (2030)
FeatureDetails
Revenue (2019)CA$2.42 billion
Revenue (2022-23)CA$8.27 billion (projected)
Household Impact80% get more in rebates than they pay in higher costs
Net PayersOnly top 20% of households pay slightly more

WHY THIS DESIGN WORKS

  1. Cooperative federalism: Feds set floor, provinces decide what works best
  2. Revenue neutrality: Provinces keep their revenue; households get rebates
  3. Popular support: 75% of Canadians supported cap-and-trade; 50%+ supported carbon tax
  4. Industry-specific OBPS: Protects against carbon leakage (firms relocating to avoid tax)
  5. Broad coalition: Even Shell Canada and Suncor endorsed the policy

POLICY DESIGN PRINCIPLES

Carbon Tax
  • Sets price of carbon
  • Predictable costs for business
  • Emissions reduction uncertain
  • Canada's chosen approach
Cap-and-Trade
  • Sets quantity of emissions
  • Predictable emissions target
  • Price can be volatile
  • EU and California's approach

KEY INSIGHT

Successful environmental policy requires political design as much as economic design. Canada's carbon tax worked because it respected provincial autonomy, returned revenue to households, and was supported by both industry and a majority of voters.

RECIPROCAL METHOD (LINEAR EQUATIONS)

3 Steps:
1. Express support dept costs as linear equations
2. Solve equations to get complete reciprocated costs
3. Allocate reciprocated costs based on usage %

Phoenix Partners Example:
AS = 600,000 + 0.10 × IS
IS = 2,400,000 + 0.25 × AS
Solve simultaneously for complete allocation

JOINT COST ALLOCATION

  • Joint Costs: Costs of a production process that yields multiple products simultaneously
  • Split-off Point: The moment products become distinguishable
  • Separable Costs: Costs incurred after split-off, assignable to specific products
  • Joint Products: Multiple outputs with high sales value
  • Byproducts: Outputs with low sales value relative to other products

JOINT COST ALLOCATION METHODS

MANAGERIAL ACCOUNTING - Session 6
January 29 | Capacity Analysis, Denominator Levels & Pricing

WHY CAPACITY MATTERS FOR COSTING & PRICING

  • The denominator used to calculate overhead rates determines the cost per unit
  • Cost per unit directly feeds into pricing decisions
  • Choosing the wrong capacity level can lead to overpricing → lost demand → even higher unit costs

FOUR DENOMINATOR-LEVEL CAPACITY CONCEPTS

LevelDefinitionBest Use
TheoreticalMaximum/ideal capacity: full efficiency all the timeBenchmarking; rarely used for costing
PracticalTheoretical minus unavoidable interruptions (maintenance, breakdowns)Product costing & capacity management; short-term pricing
NormalAverage capacity utilization satisfying demand over several periodsLong-run pricing; external reporting
Master-BudgetExpected capacity utilization for the next budget period (1 year)Performance evaluation

CAPACITY LEVEL RECOMMENDATIONS BY PURPOSE

PurposeRecommended Level
Product Costing & Capacity ManagementPractical
Short-Term PricingPractical
Long-Run PricingNormal
Performance EvaluationMaster-Budget
External ReportingNormal
Tax RequirementsActual

THE DOWNWARD DEMAND SPIRAL

Definition: A continuing reduction in demand that occurs when a firm uses demand-based capacity (master-budget or normal) to allocate fixed costs.

Mechanism:
  1. Demand drops → fewer units to absorb fixed overhead
  2. Fixed cost per unit rises → prices must increase
  3. Higher prices cause more customers to leave → demand drops further
  4. Cycle repeats until the business collapses
Prevention: Use practical capacity for costing. This reveals the cost of unused capacity as a separate line item rather than hiding it in product costs.

PRICE DETERMINATION FRAMEWORK

Price has a boundary — you can't raise it indefinitely when costs rise. Improving profit means working on the cost side:
  • Value-Added Costs: Transform the product, done right the first time, customer would pay for it
  • Non-Value-Added Essential: Necessary but don't add value (admin, compliance) → reduce
  • Waste: No value added, resources wasted → eliminate aggressively
THUNDER BOLT INC. EXERCISE
Motorcycle manufacturer, G36 model. Budgeted fixed MOH: $6,480,000. Price: $8,200/unit
Theoretical: $6,480,000 / 5,400 = $1,200/unit
Practical: $6,480,000 / 3,840 = $1,688/unit
Normal: $6,480,000 / 3,240 = $2,000/unit
Master-Budget: $6,480,000 / 3,600 = $1,800/unit
Lesson: The same $6.48M in fixed costs produces vastly different unit costs ($1,200 to $2,000) depending on the denominator chosen.

KEY INSIGHT

Using demand-based capacity levels for pricing creates a dangerous feedback loop. When demand falls, unit costs rise, forcing price increases that drive away more customers. Use practical capacity to reveal unused capacity costs separately.

BGS - Session 6
January 28 | Water Privatization & Public-Private Partnerships (Aguas del Aconquija)

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs): Combining public-service objectives with private-sector efficiency
  • Concession Contracts: Transfer management AND investment/financing responsibility to private operator
  • Bilateral Investment Treaties (BITs): International agreements protecting foreign investors, allowing them to sue governments through arbitration (ICSID)
  • Political Risk: The danger that government policy changes undermine investment viability
AGUAS DEL ACONQUIJA SA: PRIVATIZATION IN TROUBLED WATERS
Context: Argentina's 1990s privatization wave under Menem; Tucumán province water system awarded to France's CGE (Vivendi) in 1994
The Problem:
  • Only 70.5% of Tucumán had access to drinking water; 29% connected to sewerage
  • State-run DIPOS had 2,000+ agents for 1,000 connections (6× overstaffed vs. Chile)
  • Average monthly bill: US$13.22 (unrealistically low); "culture of non-payment"
  • 30-year concession awarded to CGE with $384.5M investment commitment
What Went Wrong:
  • 104% price hike implemented immediately (68% tariff + taxes + regulatory levy)
  • Brown water incident (manganese + chlorine from old dam) destroyed public trust
  • New populist governor (Bussi) demanded renegotiation; Peronist opposition fanned unrest
  • Revenue recovery dropped to 10-14% of invoicing; company losing US$2.8M/month
  • CGE filed US$300M ICSID claim against Argentina; later increased to US$700M
Outcome: Concession terminated in 1998; water system re-nationalized; no investment made since mid-1990s

KEY TENSIONS IN WATER PRIVATIZATION

IssueChallenge
Infrastructure InvestmentPaid through higher tariffs → existing users subsidize expansion to poor areas
Political RiskElected officials can unilaterally change terms; regulatory bodies captured by former state employees
Investor ProtectionBITs allow companies to sue sovereign governments, raising sovereignty concerns
Public Goods vs. MarketsIs water an essential service that should remain in state hands, or can markets deliver better outcomes?

PROPOSED SOLUTIONS FROM EXPERTS

  • Keep water fully public (Lozano)
  • Free up prices and let markets work (Urbiztondo)
  • Keep privatization but limit state role to extending to poor areas (Shargrodsky)
  • Replace concession contracts with management contracts where state retains investment responsibility (Schwarzer)
  • Differentiate tariffs by income level (Casarín)
  • Involve communities and unions in management (Zanabria)

KEY INSIGHT

Privatization of essential services requires robust institutional frameworks and political stability. When private operators must finance infrastructure through price hikes to a poor population, the political and social backlash can be devastating. The gap between investor expectations and political reality is where PPPs fail.

BGS - Session 7
January 29 | Nike Labor Practices & NGO-Corporate Strategy

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Global Supply Chain Responsibility: Can firms outsource manufacturing without outsourcing moral responsibility?
  • NGO Strategy: How nongovernmental organizations pressure multinational corporations
  • Corporate-NGO Partnerships: Turning adversaries into allies for competitive advantage
  • Brand Vulnerability: The stronger the brand, the juicier the target for activist campaigns
NIKE: HITTING THE WALL
Company: World's largest athletic footwear brand, 40%+ US market share, $9B+ revenue
Business Model: Zero in-house manufacturing; outsource to low-cost countries, invest savings in marketing
Timeline of Crisis:
  • 1980s-90s: Production shifted from Japan → Korea/Taiwan → China/Indonesia (chasing low costs)
  • 1991: Indonesian minimum wage $1/day vs. US shoe manufacturing ~$8/hour
  • 1991-97: Wave of labor unrest; Jeff Ballinger's "one company-one country" activist strategy targeted Nike
  • 1997: Worker death in Vietnamese factory; mainstream media coverage (Time, Business Week, Doonesbury)
  • Initial response: "We don't make shoes" — refused responsibility for contractor behavior
Key Lesson: Nike's outsourcing strategy created strategic vulnerability — the same brand power that drove sales amplified the reputational damage from labor abuses.
TURNING GADFLIES INTO ALLIES (Yaziji, HBR 2004)
Thesis: Partnering with NGOs is more productive than fighting them
4 NGO Strengths Companies Should Leverage:
StrengthDescription
LegitimacyPublic trusts NGO spokespeople more than CEOs or PR reps
Awareness of Social ForcesNGOs detect emerging issues before they hit mainstream
Distinct NetworksGlobal coalitions can mobilize within days ("swarming")
Technical Expertise50% of staff at major NGOs have master's/law degrees; 10-20% have PhDs
Examples of NGO Power:
  • Aventis: Forced to spend $500M buying back StarLink corn
  • Pharma companies: Withdrew lawsuit against South Africa's AIDS drug law
  • Shell Brent Spar: Greenpeace forced reversal of deep-sea disposal plan

STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS

  • Companies at highest risk: high brand visibility + consumer-facing + global operations
  • NGO attacks target production methods and spillover effects, not product attributes
  • Partnership benefits: avoid costly conflicts, gain early warning of issues, access specialized knowledge
  • The number of globally-focused NGOs quadrupled in the past decade

KEY INSIGHT

In a globalized economy, outsourcing production does not outsource responsibility. Companies must proactively manage their supply chain ethics or face reputational damage that undermines their competitive advantage.

WHAT IS A PROJECT?

"A temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result" (PMI)

  • Temporary: Has defined start and end
  • Unique: Always different from previous work (unlike operations)
  • Uncertain: Complexity and unknowns must be managed
  • Constrained: Limited time, resources, and budget

PIE CHART: ALMOST ALWAYS A BAD CHOICE

"The deadly sin #10 of presenting numbers: Using a pie chart — period." (Randall Bolten)
  • Bad at showing trends (in % or absolute values)
  • Difficult to compare sectors accurately
  • 3D effects, too many slices, and wrong ordering make them worse
  • Almost always a better alternative exists (bar chart, table)

DATA VISUALIZATION BEST PRACTICES

  1. Eliminate Chartjunk (Tufte): Remove any visual element not necessary to understand the data
  2. Maximize Data-Ink Ratio (Tufte): Every pixel should represent data, not decoration
  3. Only relevant information: No background images, excessive colors, 3D effects, or grid lines
  4. Visual differences = data differences: Limit visual variety to points you want to highlight
  5. Proportional representation: Visual differences should match actual data differences

KEY INSIGHT

Good data visualization is about clarity, not decoration. The goal is to make patterns visible and comparisons easy, while eliminating anything that distracts from the data story.
