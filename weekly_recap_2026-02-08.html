DATA ANALYTICS FOR MANAGERS

Data Analytics - Sessions 7 & 8
Feb 2 & 4 | Risk Analysis Practice Workshops

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Monte Carlo Simulation: Technique for understanding impact of risk and uncertainty using random sampling to generate probability distributions of outcomes
  • @Risk Software: Excel add-in for risk analysis using Monte Carlo simulation - essential tool for quantitative risk assessment
  • Sensitivity Analysis: Identifying which input variables have the greatest impact on output uncertainty
  • Risk Metrics: VaR (Value at Risk), expected value, standard deviation of outcomes

KEY FORMULAS

Expected Value: E[X] = Sum of (probability x outcome)

Standard Deviation: Measures spread of possible outcomes around expected value

Coefficient of Variation: CV = Standard Deviation / Mean (for comparing relative risk)

PRACTICAL APPLICATION

Risk Analysis Workshop
Focus: Using @Risk to model uncertainty in business decisions
Key Steps: Define distributions for uncertain inputs, run simulations, interpret tornado charts and sensitivity analysis

MANAGERIAL ECONOMICS

Managerial Economics - Sessions 6 & 7
Feb 3 & 6 | Carbon Pricing & Environmental Policy

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Carbon Tax: Price per ton of CO2 emissions - internalizes the externality of pollution, making emitters pay for environmental costs
  • Cap-and-Trade: Sets quantitative emission limits (cap) and allows trading of permits - price varies based on market
  • Cooperative Federalism: Federal government sets floor standards; states/provinces choose implementation - Canada's approach
  • Output-Based Pricing System (OBPS): Only charges for emissions exceeding industry benchmarks - prevents carbon leakage
CASE: Canada's Federalist Carbon Tax
Context: Trudeau's 2015 campaign promised carbon pricing; law passed 2018
Design: Provinces can design own systems meeting federal floor OR adopt federal backstop
Revenue: Stays in province - 80% of households get more back in rebates than they pay
Price Path: CA$10/ton (2018) rising to CA$170/ton by 2030
Key Insight: Political success came from letting provinces keep revenue and choose implementation

POLICY DESIGN LESSONS

  • Lesson 1: Set floor, let jurisdictions decide implementation details
  • Lesson 2: Keep revenue local to avoid "tax grab" perception
  • Lesson 3: Emphasize benefits (rebates) over costs (taxes)
  • Lesson 4: Industrial pricing may be easier than consumer fuel taxes
Follow-up Questions for Self-Assessment
  1. What is the fundamental difference between a carbon tax and cap-and-trade system?
  2. How does output-based pricing prevent carbon leakage?

BUSINESS, GOVERNMENT & SUSTAINABILITY

BGS - Session 8
Feb 4 | Corporate Social Responsibility & Creating Shared Value

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Shared Value: Policies and practices that enhance competitiveness while advancing economic and social conditions in communities - NOT charity or CSR
  • CSR vs. CSV: CSR is peripheral/discretionary; CSV is integral to profitability and competitive positioning
  • Three Ways to Create Shared Value:
    1. Reconceiving products and markets (meeting societal needs)
    2. Redefining productivity in the value chain (resource efficiency, employee wellness)
    3. Enabling local cluster development (supplier capability, infrastructure)
CASE: Creating Shared Value (Porter & Kramer)
Core Argument: Capitalism is under siege because companies optimize short-term profits while ignoring societal needs
Solution: Redefine corporate purpose around creating shared value - economic value through societal value
Examples:
  • Nestl√© Nespresso: Improved coffee farmer productivity by 300%+ through better practices, securing supply while increasing farmer incomes
  • Johnson & Johnson: Saved $250M on healthcare costs through employee wellness programs
  • Walmart: Cut 100M miles from truck routes, saved $200M AND reduced emissions

KEY INSIGHT

The concept of shared value recognizes that societal needs, not just conventional economic needs, define markets. Social harms create internal costs for firms - pollution wastes resources, poor employee health reduces productivity, weak suppliers threaten quality.

MANAGERIAL ACCOUNTING

Managerial Accounting - Session 7
Feb 5 | Activity-Based Costing

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Activity-Based Costing (ABC): Assigns overhead costs based on activities that drive costs, not just volume measures
  • Cost Drivers: Factors that cause costs to be incurred (machine setups, inspections, purchase orders)
  • Traditional vs. ABC: Traditional uses volume-based allocation (labor hours, machine hours); ABC uses multiple activity-based drivers
  • Cost Pool: Grouping of individual costs associated with an activity

KEY FORMULAS

Activity Rate: Total Activity Cost Pool / Total Activity Driver Units

Cost Assignment: Activity Rate x Activity Driver Units Used

Total Product Cost (ABC): Direct Materials + Direct Labor + Sum of (Activity Rate x Activity Driver Units)
Follow-up Questions for Self-Assessment
  1. What problem does Activity-Based Costing solve that traditional costing methods cannot?
  2. How might implementing ABC change a company's view of product profitability?

Reactive (During/After)

  • Demand shifting/rationing

  • Production rescheduling

CORPORATE FINANCE

Corporate Finance - Session 8
Feb 6 | Free Cash Flows

KEY CONCEPTS

  • Free Cash Flow (FCF): Cash available to all capital providers after operating expenses and investments
  • Relevant Cash Flows: Only incremental, after-tax cash flows matter for investment decisions
  • Sunk Costs: Past expenditures that cannot be recovered - EXCLUDE from analysis
  • Opportunity Costs: Value of next-best alternative - INCLUDE in analysis
  • Cannibalization: When new product reduces sales of existing products - INCLUDE the lost contribution

KEY FORMULAS

Free Cash Flow: EBIT(1-t) + Depreciation - CapEx - Change in Working Capital

Incremental Cash Flow: CF(with project) - CF(without project)

Working Capital: Current Assets - Current Liabilities (typically: Receivables + Inventory - Payables)
CASE: The New Line Project
Context: Juan Navarro analyzing investment in new DIY tool line at Herramientas Industriales
Key Issues to Resolve:
  • Should cannibalization of old line sales be included? (YES - incremental analysis)
  • Should allocated overhead be included? (NO - not incremental)
  • Should idle capacity cost be charged? (NO - sunk/opportunity cost debate)
  • Working capital needs: 25% Y1, 22% Y2, 20% Y3+
Investment: 300,000 machinery + working capital; Required return: 25%
Follow-up Questions for Self-Assessment
  1. Why should sunk costs be excluded from project cash flow analysis?
  2. Should cannibalization effects be included in project analysis? Why?
  3. How does working capital affect project cash flows?
